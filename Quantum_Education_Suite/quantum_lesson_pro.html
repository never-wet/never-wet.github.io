The Schrödinger Equation

Think of this as the "F = ma" of the subatomic world. It predicts where a particle will be, but only as a probability.

01 The Simple Analogy

If you kick a football, Newton tells you exactly where it lands. In the quantum world, particles are "fuzzy." The Wavefunction ($\Psi$) is a cloud representing every possible place a particle could be. Schrödinger's equation is the math that tells the cloud how to move.

How to Remember it (Mnemonic)

"I Have To Change PSI to Have PSI."

  • I ($) = The complex plane
  • Have ($\hbar$) = Planck's Constant
  • To Change ($\frac{\partial}{\partial t}$) = Time derivative
  • PSI ($\Psi$) = The wave itself

02 The Symbol Breakdown

The Hamiltonian ($\hat{H}$)

This is the "Energy Master." It adds up the particle's Kinetic Energy (speed) and Potential Energy (gravity/electricity). If you want to know the energy of a particle, you "apply" the Hamiltonian to it.

H-Bar ($\hbar$)

This is Planck's constant divided by \pi$. It is the "resolution" of the universe. Without it, the subatomic world would behave just like the macro world.

Exam Survival Guide

  • 1. Check Units! Ensure your energy units ($) match the Hamiltonian output.
  • 2. Normalization Remember: The total probability of a particle being *somewhere* must be 1. Don't forget to normalize your wavefunctions!
  • 3. Time vs. Time-Independent Read the question carefully. If the energy doesn't change over time, use the shorter version: $\hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi$.
